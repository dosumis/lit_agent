## Markers for Distinguishing Astrocyte Cell Types in the Mouse Brain

Astrocytes are a highly heterogeneous population of glial cells in the central nervous system, and recent advances in single-cell transcriptomics have revealed remarkable molecular diversity among these cells. Understanding the markers that distinguish different astrocyte subtypes is essential for studying their specialized functions in various brain regions.

### Pan-Astrocyte Markers

**Classical Structural Markers**

Several markers label most or all astrocytes in the mouse brain, though with varying degrees of completeness and regional specificity:[1][2][3][4][5]

**GFAP (Glial Fibrillary Acidic Protein)** is the most widely used astrocyte marker, being an intermediate filament protein that forms the astrocytic cytoskeleton. However, GFAP expression is heterogeneous across brain regions—it is highly expressed in the hippocampus and white matter but shows lower expression in cortical gray matter. In healthy cortical gray matter, only a subset of astrocytes express detectable GFAP levels, making it an incomplete pan-astrocyte marker.[6][3][4][5][7][8][1]

**ALDH1L1 (Aldehyde Dehydrogenase 1 Family Member L1)** is considered the most comprehensive pan-astrocyte marker, labeling virtually all astrocytes throughout the CNS with substantially broader coverage than GFAP. This folate enzyme is involved in metabolism and shows high specificity for astrocytes, though some expression has been reported in oligodendrocytes under certain conditions.[9][5][10][11]

**S100β** is a calcium-binding protein expressed predominantly in gray matter astrocytes, complementing GFAP which is more abundant in white matter. S100β preferentially labels astrocyte cell bodies with less extensive branching patterns. However, it also labels oligodendrocytes and oligodendrocyte progenitor cells, limiting its specificity.[2][3][5][12][1]

**Functional Markers**

**Glutamate Transporters**: Two sodium-dependent glutamate transporters are highly expressed in astrocytes:[13][14][15][16][17]
- **GLT-1 (EAAT2 in humans; Slc1a2)** accounts for over 90% of glutamate uptake in the brain and is predominantly expressed in astrocyte processes, particularly in hippocampus, cortex, and striatum. GLT-1 shows regional variation, with higher expression in hippocampus and cerebral cortex compared to other regions.[18][19][15][16][20][13]
- **GLAST (EAAT1 in humans; Slc1a3)** is the other major astrocytic glutamate transporter, expressed more uniformly across brain regions.[21][10][15][16][17][20]

**Glutamine Synthetase (GS)** is a highly reliable astrocyte marker that converts glutamate to glutamine, covering nearly all astrocyte subtypes. It is considered one of the most general astrocyte markers, though some expression in oligodendrocytes has been reported.[22][23][24][25][1]

**Aquaporin-4 (AQP4)** is a water channel protein concentrated in astrocyte endfeet contacting blood vessels. While important for astrocyte function in water homeostasis, it is also expressed by ependymal cells.[4][5][10][26][6]

**Connexins** form gap junction channels between astrocytes. Connexin 43 (Cx43, encoded by Gja1) and Connexin 30 (Cx30, encoded by Gjb6) are astrocyte-specific and important for intercellular communication.[10][26][6][4]

**Kir4.1 (Kcnj10)** is an inwardly rectifying potassium channel critical for potassium spatial buffering and maintaining the astrocyte resting membrane potential. It co-localizes with AQP4 in astrocyte membranes and endfeet.[27][28][29][30][31]

**Rab6A** has been identified as a pan-astrocytic marker in both mouse and human brain, showing massive and diffuse expression in peripheral astrocyte processes.[6]

**NDRG2** and S100β show more uniform distribution than GFAP throughout the mouse cerebrum, making them useful complementary markers.[3][1][2]

### Regional Astrocyte Markers

**White Matter vs. Gray Matter Astrocytes**

Astrocytes in white matter (fibrous astrocytes) and gray matter (protoplasmic astrocytes) are biochemically and developmentally distinct:[32][33]

- **Fibrous astrocytes** in white matter express high levels of GFAP and have a characteristic stellate morphology with long, thin processes.[5][7][32]
- **Protoplasmic astrocytes** in gray matter typically show lower GFAP expression but higher S100β, with bushy morphology and fine leaflet-like processes.[7][34][1][5][32]

**Hippocampus vs. Striatum**

Regional molecular differences have been documented between hippocampal and striatal astrocytes:[35]
- **Striatal astrocytes** show enriched expression of μ-crystallin (Crym), involved in thyroid hormone regulation, and have larger territorial volumes.[35]
- **Hippocampal astrocytes** show highest expression of GFAP among the marker genes common between these regions and display stronger physical interactions with excitatory synapses.[35]

### Cortical Layer-Specific Astrocyte Markers

Recent spatial transcriptomic studies have revealed that cortical astrocytes organize into distinct laminar patterns that differ from the six neuronal layers:[36][37][38][39]

**Superficial Layer Astrocytes** (layers I-II/III) express markers including:
- Higher levels of genes involved in synaptic ensheathment[38][39]
- Specific expression of Mfge8 (milk fat globule-EGF factor 8)[40][41]

**Mid-Layer Astrocytes** are enriched in layer V[37][39]

**Deep Layer Astrocytes** (layer VI) show:
- Distinct morphologies with differences in cell orientation and territorial volume[39][38]
- Different gene expression profiles from upper-layer astrocytes[38][39]

Layer-specific differences in astrocyte properties depend on neuronal cues, as they are abolished in *Reeler* and *Dab1* conditional knockout mice where neuronal layering is disrupted.[37][39][38]

### Transcriptionally Defined Astrocyte Subtypes

Single-cell RNA sequencing has identified five major transcriptomically distinct astrocyte subtypes (AST1-5) in the adult mouse cortex and hippocampus:[42][43][44][40]

**AST1 Subtype**:
- Located in the subpial layer and hippocampus
- Expresses high levels of Gfap and Agt (angiotensinogen)[43][36]
- Represents mature astrocytes[42]

**AST2 Subtype**:
- High expression in cortical layers 2/3 and 5, lower in layers 1, 4, and 6
- Negligible hippocampal expression
- Characterized by expression of Unc13c[36][43]
- Linked to glutamatergic neurotransmission[43]

**AST3 Subtype**:
- Distributed throughout cortical layers and hippocampus, prevalent in layer VI
- Distinguished by lack of Gfap expression[43]
- Associated with GABAergic neurotransmission[43]

**AST4 Subtype**:
- Found predominantly in the subgranular zone (SGZ) of the hippocampal dentate gyrus
- Highly expresses genes associated with cell fate specification (Ascl1) and morphogenesis (Frzb)[45][42]
- May represent a progenitor or neural stem cell population[40][45][42]

**AST5 Subtype**:
- Expression overlaps substantially with AST4
- Hypothesized to represent an intermediate state between progenitor and mature astrocyte[42]
- Shows enrichment in cortical layers 2/3 and 5, stratum lacunosum-moleculare, dentate gyrus, and subpial layers[42]

### Interlaminar Astrocytes

In primate cortex (including human), a specialized population called **interlaminar astrocytes (ILAs)** exists in layer I with long processes extending through multiple cortical layers. These astrocytes express:[46][47]
- GFAP
- Radial glial markers: Pax6, Sox2, Nestin
- Astrocyte markers: S100β, Aqp4, GLAST
- Specific markers: Cryab, Hopx (in primates)[46]

Mouse cortex has only rudimentary ILAs that lack some of these markers, representing a species-specific difference.[47][46]

### Developmental and Transcription Factor Markers

**Developmental Astrocyte Markers**:
- **Vimentin** is an intermediate filament protein involved in forming astrocyte morphology, expressed during development and in reactive astrocytes[26][48][10]
- **Nestin** is expressed in neural progenitor cells and developing astrocytes[49]
- **CD44** starts expression around E13.5 in the developing mouse spinal cord and marks astrocyte-restricted lineage cells[50][51][52]

**Transcription Factors**:
Multiple transcription factors are expressed in astrocyte subtypes and regulate their development and regional identity:[53][54][55]

- **SOX9** is highly specific to astrocytes and regulates astrocyte differentiation[56][26]
- **NFIA, NFIB, NFIX** (Nuclear Factor I family) are expressed in >80% of astrocytes across brain regions and are critical for the gliogenic switch and maintenance of astrocyte identity[54][55][57][10][56]
- **PAX6** is expressed in dorsal astrocyte subtypes (VA1) and regulates GLT-1 expression[58][53]
- **NKX6.1** is expressed in ventral astrocyte subtypes (VA2, VA3) in the spinal cord[59][60][53]
- **Reelin** and **SLIT1** are expressed in positionally distinct astrocyte subtypes in the spinal cord[53]

### Cerebellar Bergmann Glia Markers

Bergmann glia are specialized radial astrocytes in the cerebellum with distinct marker expression:[61][62][63][64]
- Classical astrocyte markers: Slc1a3 (GLAST), Fabp7, S100β
- Bergmann glia-specific markers in adult cerebellum: Luzp2, Gpr89, Lgi4, Gdf10[63][64][61]
- NPY-GFP labels Bergmann glia in a zonally organized pattern[62]

### Reactive Astrocyte Markers

Upon injury, disease, or inflammation, astrocytes undergo reactive gliosis with distinct molecular signatures:[65][66][67][68][69]

**A1 (Neurotoxic) Reactive Astrocytes** express:
- Complement components: C3, C1q, C4b[68][69][65]
- Serpina3n (Serpin Family A Member 3)[67][69][65][68]
- Cxcl10 (chemokine)[69][68]

**A2 (Neuroprotective) Reactive Astrocytes** express:
- S100a10
- Sphk1
- Different cytokine profiles[70]

**Pan-Reactive Markers**:
- **Lipocalin-2 (Lcn2)** is strongly upregulated in reactive astrocytes following injury[71][72][67][68]
- **GFAP** is upregulated in reactive astrocytes, though upregulation varies by brain region[66][4][5][67]
- **Vimentin** increases in reactive astrocytes[5]
- **CD44** is upregulated in reactive astrocytes and correlates with loss of protoplasmic astrocyte markers[51][50]

### Markers of Astrocyte Metabolic and Secretory Functions

**SPARC (Secreted Protein Acidic and Rich in Cysteine)** is an extracellular matrix protein secreted by astrocytes that regulates synapse formation. SPARC and its related protein SPARCL1 mark distinct astrocyte progenitor subgroups during early gliogenesis.[73][74][75]

**Cholesterol Metabolism Markers**: Astrocytes express genes involved in cholesterol synthesis and transport, which is essential for synapse formation and maintenance.[76][77]

### Regional Diversity: Thalamus, Hypothalamus, and Brainstem

Astrocytes in subcortical regions show additional diversity:
- **Thalamic astrocytes** differ in glutamatergic receptor expression, with subpopulations showing heterogeneous responses to kainate and AMPA receptor modulators[78]
- **Brainstem astrocytes** show selective expression of Nkx6.1, unlike cortical and hippocampal astrocytes[55][54]
- **Diencephalic astrocytes** show molecular diversity with seven distinct gene expression clusters[79]

### Practical Considerations for Marker Selection

When selecting markers to identify astrocytes in the mouse brain, several factors should be considered:[80][12][1][4][5]

1. **Regional heterogeneity**: GFAP is more abundant in hippocampus and white matter; S100β is more prominent in cortex and gray matter
2. **Completeness**: ALDH1L1 labels the broadest astrocyte population
3. **Developmental stage**: GFAP is typically more abundant in mature astrocytes
4. **Pathological state**: CD44, Lcn2, and Serpina3n are upregulated in reactive astrocytes
5. **Functional questions**: Use glutamate transporters (GLT-1, GLAST), glutamine synthetase, or Kir4.1 for studies of synaptic function and metabolism
6. **Multiple markers**: Combining markers (e.g., GFAP + S100β + ALDH1L1) provides the most comprehensive astrocyte identification

### Summary

The markers available for distinguishing astrocyte cell types in the mouse brain range from pan-astrocytic markers like ALDH1L1 and glutamine synthetase to region-specific markers, layer-specific markers, and subtype-specific transcriptomic signatures. The field has progressed from a binary classification (protoplasmic vs. fibrous) to recognizing at least five major transcriptomically distinct subtypes (AST1-5) with additional layer-specific, regional, and functional specializations. Recent single-cell and spatial transcriptomic approaches continue to reveal increasing levels of astrocyte diversity, with implications for understanding their specialized roles in neural circuits, development, aging, and disease.[81][44][76][65][7][32][40]
